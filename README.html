Why Parametric Insurance — Not Traditional
Traditional insurance indemnifies actual loss — requiring documentation, loss assessment, and manual approval. Parametric insurance pays on the occurrence of a pre-defined event, regardless of individual loss documentation. This model is uniquely suited to gig workers because it eliminates every friction point that makes traditional insurance unusable for this population: no claim to file, no assessor to wait for, no proof to gather. The event itself — objectively measured — is the trigger.

What Makes This Architecturally Novel

The central innovation in GigShield is solving the platform API gap. Delivery platforms like Zomato and Swiggy do not expose rider activity APIs to third parties. GigShield resolves this through a consent-based multi-signal activity model that infers working status from GPS movement patterns, passive sensor readings (network type, accelerometer pattern, ambient sound level), and restaurant-zone proximity — all collected passively in the background by the Flutter app. No platform cooperation is required, and no single signal can be trivially gamed in isolation.

06 — Premium Calculation Engine

AI-Driven Personalised Pricing — Fully Traced

GigShield does not use a fixed lookup table. Every rider receives a personalised weekly premium computed by a hybrid AI risk model combining historical disruption data with 7-day predictive forecasts. Here is the full methodology, formula, and three worked examples end-to-end.

The Hybrid Risk Model — Historical + Forecast

The core innovation in GigShield's pricing engine is that it does not rely on historical data alone, nor on forecast data alone. It combines both through a weighted hybrid formula. Historical data tells us what a zone's long-run risk profile is. Forecast data tells us what is likely to happen in the specific week the rider is buying coverage. Neither is sufficient on its own — a zone with high historical risk may have a clear week ahead, and a low-risk zone may face an unusual storm forecast. The hybrid model captures both dimensions.

Historical Risk Score (60% weight)
Derived from 3 years of IMD pin-code level weather records, CPCB AQI logs, and government civic disruption records. Represents the long-run base rate of disruption for the rider's zone. Stable week-to-week. Updated monthly as new claims data accumulates.

A zone in coastal Mumbai with 28 disruption events per year scores much higher than an elevated Pune zone with 6 events per year.
Forecast Risk Score (40% weight)
Derived from the OpenWeather 7-day forecast API. Converts the coming week's weather predictions into a risk score between 0.0 and 1.0. A clear week with low rain probability scores near 0.05. A week with a heavy storm system forecast scores near 0.72.

This is what makes the premium dynamic — the same zone can have a materially different premium in a clear week versus a monsoon week.
THE HYBRID RISK FORMULA
( 0.6 × Historical Risk Score ) + ( 0.4 × Forecast Risk Score ) = Final Disruption Risk %
The 60/40 weighting reflects a deliberate design choice: historical data is more reliable as a base rate but does not capture near-term events. Forecast data is highly responsive but noisier over longer windows. The blend produces a stable-yet-responsive disruption probability that neither overreacts to short-term forecasts nor ignores them.

08 — Adversarial Defense

Anti-Spoofing Strategy

GigShield uses a two-tier fraud architecture. The device layer defeats opportunistic individual fraud. The system layer defeats coordinated syndicate attacks. Each tier targets a different attacker profile. Neither tier relies on the other.

The Threat: Organized syndicates coordinate via Telegram. Members install GPS spoofing applications that simulate realistic delivery-zone movement — gradual, plausible routes through known restaurant clusters. During a red-alert weather event, 500+ riders activate simultaneously. The payout system sees hundreds of "active" riders in the disruption zone. The liquidity pool drains in minutes.

The honest position: GPS spoofing at the device level cannot be fully stopped — telecom companies do not expose cell tower data directly to third-party apps, and sophisticated spoofing tools can simulate realistic movement. GigShield does not overclaim device-level detection. Instead, the primary defense against syndicates operates at the system level — using signals that no individual rider or group of riders can manipulate from their phone.

8.2 — Passive Sensor Verification Layer (Device Layer)

For fraud that passes the basic movement checks — someone who walks outside or uses a moderate spoofing tool — GigShield adds a passive sensor verification layer using three signals available in the Flutter app that together cover three independent physical dimensions. A fraudster would need to simultaneously satisfy all three to pass, which functionally means they would need to actually be working.

Why These Three Signals — Not More
Implementing three signals well is far more valuable than attempting six partially. These three were chosen because they are technically achievable using standard Flutter APIs, cover three independent physical dimensions (network environment, physical motion, acoustic environment), and collectively defeat the opportunistic fraudster without producing false-positives on genuine riders experiencing poor weather conditions.
SIGNAL 01

Network Type Check — navigator.connection API

The simplest and most reliable signal. Checks whether the device is connected to mobile data or a saved home Wi-Fi network at the time of a trigger event. A rider actively delivering in a disruption zone will almost always be on mobile data. A rider at home will almost always be on their home Wi-Fi network.

Implementation requires two lines of code using the navigator.connection API. Works on Android and iOS with no additional permissions beyond those already granted for GPS tracking.

// Implementation const conn = navigator.connection; const isOnMobileData = conn.type === 'cellular'; // Scoring mobile data → activity score +15 points home Wi-Fi → fraud indicator flagged
SIGNAL 02

Accelerometer Pattern — DeviceMotion API

The strongest device-level signal. Uses the DeviceMotion API available in all Flutter contexts. During an active shift, the app samples accelerometer readings every 30 seconds. A two-wheeler in urban traffic produces a distinctive pattern — irregular vibration, stop-start acceleration, and turning forces. A person sitting at home produces a flat or near-zero variance pattern.

The check is not complex ML — just a variance threshold. If the standard deviation of accelerometer readings over a 5-minute window is below a threshold consistent with stationary behaviour, the signal flags low activity confidence.

// Sample every 30 seconds during active shift accel_readings = DeviceMotion.acceleration (sampled over 5 min) variance = stddev(accel_readings) variance > threshold → consistent with vehicle movement variance < threshold → consistent with stationary device
SIGNAL 03

Ambient Sound Level — Web Audio API

Uses the Web Audio API to measure ambient decibel level only — no audio is recorded or stored, just a numeric reading. A food delivery zone during peak hours reads measurably louder than a quiet residential area. The app samples once every 2 minutes during a shift and logs the average level.

This is fully privacy-safe: no audio data leaves the device. Only the decibel number is transmitted to the backend. Implementation is achievable in one day and provides a third dimension of physical environment verification that is completely independent of GPS and motion.

// Sample ambient level every 2 minutes — no recording level_db = AudioContext → AnalyserNode → getByteFrequencyData() level_db > delivery_zone_threshold → outdoor environment signal level_db < quiet_home_threshold → residential environment signal

These three signals are named collectively as the Passive Sensor Verification Layer. The name is accurate — all three signals are collected passively with no rider interaction required — and precisely describes the mechanism: physical environment verification through device sensors rather than GPS coordinates alone.

8.3 — Syndicate Defense: Why the Real Protection is at the System Level

The device layer stops the individual couch claimer and moderate spoofer. It does not stop a sophisticated syndicate where 500 people walk outside simultaneously and pass all three sensor checks. That attack is stopped by four system-level mechanisms that operate on data no individual rider or group can manipulate from their phone.

No parametric insurance platform has fully solved GPS spoofing at the device level. The goal is not to make fraud impossible — it is to make it unprofitable and high-risk. Even a successful fraudulent claim yields ₹600 once, against permanent account termination. For riders who are actually delivering, the economics of fraud simply do not make sense.

10 — System Architecture & Decision Engine

How the System Is Built

GigShield is a five-engine FastAPI backend connected to a Flutter mobile frontend. Every payout decision flows through a continuously evaluating decision engine that combines live external data with per-rider AI scoring.

The Five Internal Engines

Risk Engine
Evaluates zone-level disruption probability using IMD weather data, CPCB AQI feeds, and historical event logs. Combines historical risk scores with 7-day forecast data via the hybrid 60/40 model and updates the disruption risk percentage continuously during active alerts.
Activity Engine
Processes GPS telemetry, passive sensor readings (network type, accelerometer variance, ambient sound level), and stop-duration patterns from the Flutter app. Produces a composite activity score (0–100) per active rider in real time.
Fraud Engine
Runs the isolation forest anomaly model, contradiction scorer, zone velocity monitor, and cross-rider graph analysis. Operates in parallel with the Activity Engine — not sequentially — so syndicate detection fires as fast as individual fraud detection.
Pricing Engine
Hybrid risk model serving personalised weekly premiums — combining 60% historical disruption data with 40% 7-day forecast data. Retrains monthly on accumulated claims data. SHAP output layer generates plain-language premium breakdowns shown in the Flutter app at onboarding and each renewal.
Decision Engine
The central orchestrator. Continuously evaluates the four payout gates (disruption confirmed, activity ≥65, fraud <20%, order volume drop ≥35%). Emits a payout event when all conditions are satisfied simultaneously, or routes to the appropriate hold/review tier if they are not.
Payout System
Receives payout events from the Decision Engine. Calls the Razorpay UPI API with the rider's registered VPA. On success: updates policy ledger and triggers WhatsApp notification. On failure: retries once, then escalates to manual queue.

The Decision Engine Logic

The Decision Engine runs as a continuous evaluation loop — not a batch job. Every time any input signal changes (a new weather reading, an updated activity score, a civic alert), the engine re-evaluates all active riders in the affected zone. This is what enables the 60-second payout target: the decision is already partially evaluated before the triggering event completes.

Decision Engine — Continuous Evaluation Logic
IF ( Weather / Civic Trigger = TRUE ) API threshold breached in rider's active zone
AND ( AI Activity Score >= 65 ) Multi-signal composite from Activity Engine
AND ( Fraud Probability < 20% ) Isolation forest + contradiction score from Fraud Engine
AND ( Zone Order Volume Drop >= 35% ) Platform API proxy — confirms real disruption, not spoofed
THEN Execute Razorpay UPI Payout within 60 Seconds Ledger updated · WhatsApp sent · Audit log created
Flutter App Data Flow
The Flutter mobile app serves two roles. For riders it is the interface for onboarding, policy management, payout history, and premium payments. For the backend it is the passive telemetry source — continuously streaming GPS coordinates, network type, accelerometer readings, ambient sound levels, and device attestation status to the FastAPI backend during the rider's registered working hours. No rider interaction is required to transmit these signals; they flow automatically as background data from the app's location, motion, and network services.

Why a 70–75% Loss Ratio is Sustainable
Parametric insurance products globally operate at loss ratios between 60% and 80%, reflecting the fact that they pay on objective event occurrence rather than documented individual loss. The GigShield target of 70–75% is deliberately conservative — it allocates sufficient margin for fraud reserve, technology costs, and reinsurance. At scale, the loss ratio is expected to improve further as the hybrid risk model accumulates real claims data and zone-level historical risk scores become more accurate at the pin-code level.

Distribution Strategy

The most capital-efficient distribution channel is a direct integration with Zomato and Swiggy — offering GigShield as an opt-in weekly benefit visible within the rider earnings section of the platform app. This channel eliminates customer acquisition cost entirely and reaches riders at the moment they are most likely to value income protection: immediately after viewing their weekly earnings. Secondary channels include WhatsApp-based community onboarding through delivery partner associations and digital kiosks at high-density pickup zones such as food courts and restaurant clusters.
