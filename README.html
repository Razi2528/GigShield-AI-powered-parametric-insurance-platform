Why Parametric Insurance — Not Traditional
Traditional insurance indemnifies actual loss — requiring documentation, loss assessment, and manual approval. Parametric insurance pays on the occurrence of a pre-defined event, regardless of individual loss documentation. This model is uniquely suited to gig workers because it eliminates every friction point that makes traditional insurance unusable for this population: no claim to file, no assessor to wait for, no proof to gather. The event itself — objectively measured — is the trigger.

What Makes This Architecturally Novel

The central innovation in GigShield is solving the platform API gap. Delivery platforms like Zomato and Swiggy do not expose rider activity APIs to third parties. Previous attempts at gig worker insurance could not verify whether a rider was actually working at the time of a disruption, making automated payouts impossible without enabling mass fraud. GigShield resolves this through a consent-based multi-signal activity model that infers working status from GPS movement patterns, cell tower triangulation, network environment signals, and restaurant-zone proximity — all collected passively in the background by the Flutter app during the rider's registered working hours. No platform cooperation is required, and no single signal can be trivially gamed.

06 — Premium Calculation Engine

AI-Driven Personalised Pricing — Fully Traced

GigShield does not use a fixed lookup table. Every rider receives a personalised weekly premium computed by a two-stage XGBoost pipeline. Here is the full methodology, formula, and three worked examples end-to-end.

The Two-Stage Model Architecture

Stage 1 — Disruption Probability Classifier
Model type: XGBoost Classifier (binary output per day)

Training data: 3 years of IMD weather records by pin code, CPCB AQI logs, government gazette civic disruption records, platform order volume proxy data.

Output: P(disruption) — the probability that a disruption will occur in the rider's zone during the coming 7-day window. This is not a binary yes/no; it is a continuous probability value between 0.05 and 0.45, updated weekly as new forecast data arrives.
Stage 2 — Premium Regression Model
Model type: XGBoost Regressor (continuous output in ₹)

Input: P(disruption) from Stage 1 plus 11 additional features across zone, environment, rider profile, and temporal categories.

Output: Weekly premium in ₹, clamped between a floor of ₹49 and a ceiling of ₹249 to prevent extreme outlier premiums for either the rider or the platform.

Explainability: A SHAP layer translates model output into plain-language premium breakdowns shown to the rider at onboarding.

The Premium Formula — Step by Step

The formula has five sequential steps. Each step is a transformation applied to the output of the previous one. The final output is clamped to protect both riders (floor) and the platform (ceiling).

STEP 1
Base Expected Loss
P(disruption)  ×  ₹600
P(disruption) comes from the Stage 1 XGBoost Classifier. It is a probability between 0.05 and 0.45 representing how likely a payout-triggering event is in the rider's zone during the coming week. Multiplying by ₹600 converts probability into the expected monetary loss the platform must cover per rider.
e.g. 0.18 × ₹600 = ₹108
↓
STEP 2
Apply Operational Margin (×1.30)
Base Expected Loss  ×  1.30
A fixed 30% loading is added to cover three costs: platform technology and operations (~10%), a fraud reserve to fund contested claims and manual reviews (~10%), and a reinsurance buffer for catastrophic multi-zone events (~10%). This margin is fixed — it does not vary by rider or zone.
₹108 × 1.30 = ₹140.40
↓
STEP 3
Apply Zone Multiplier (×0.80 to ×1.30)
× Zone_Multiplier
The zone multiplier reflects the physical risk characteristics of the rider's delivery zone — elevation category, historical waterlogging incidents, proximity to rivers or coastline, and drainage infrastructure quality. A dry elevated zone with no flood history scores 0.80, reducing the premium. A low-lying coastal zone with annual flooding scores 1.30, increasing it. The AI derives this from GIS terrain data and IMD historical records.
₹140.40 × 1.00 = ₹140.40
↓
STEP 4
Apply Seasonal Loading (×1.00 or ×1.15)
× Seasonal_Loading
During India's monsoon season (June through September as defined by IMD's seasonal calendar), disruption probability across all zones increases materially. A 15% seasonal loading is applied automatically to all active policies during these months. Outside the monsoon window, the multiplier is 1.00 — no seasonal adjustment. This is a binary flag, not a gradient.
₹140.40 × 1.00 = ₹140.40 (non-monsoon)
↓
STEP 5
Subtract Loyalty Discount (₹0 to ₹15)
− Loyalty_Discount
Riders who maintain continuous coverage with no fraud flags accumulate a loyalty discount: ₹5 for each 6-month period of uninterrupted coverage, up to a maximum of ₹15 (reached at 18 months). This discount rewards long-term riders and reduces churn. It is applied as a flat rupee deduction — not a percentage — so it provides proportionally greater benefit to lower-risk riders on smaller premiums.
₹140.40 − ₹10 = ₹130.40
↓
FINAL DELIVERED PREMIUM
max( ₹49,   min( ₹249,   calculated_premium ) )
The output is clamped between a floor of ₹49 and a ceiling of ₹249. The floor ensures every policy carries enough premium to cover minimum platform costs. The ceiling prevents extreme premiums in very high-risk scenarios — protecting rider affordability. Rider C above delivers ₹130 per week.

08 — Adversarial Defense

Anti-Spoofing Strategy

A coordinated syndicate of 500 riders using GPS spoofing apps is not a hypothetical threat. It is a documented attack pattern from beta deployments of parametric platforms in India. GigShield's architecture was redesigned to defeat it.

The Attack: Organized syndicates coordinate via Telegram. Members install advanced GPS spoofing applications that simulate realistic delivery-zone movement — not teleportation, but gradual, plausible routes through known restaurant clusters. During a red-alert weather event, 500+ members activate simultaneously. The payout system, seeing hundreds of "active" riders in the disruption zone, initiates transfers. The liquidity pool drains in minutes.

Why standard defenses fail: Speed-and-jump detection catches naive spoofing. It does not catch an application that simulates a 30 km/h two-wheeler moving through a known delivery route. Isolation forest anomaly detection catches individual behavioral deviations. It does not catch 500 new accounts with no behavioral history to deviate from. Simple GPS verification is officially obsolete.

7.1 — The Differentiation: How the AI Tells a Real Rider from a Bad Actor

GigShield cross-validates four independent data streams before authorising any payout. A spoofing application can falsify GPS coordinates. It cannot simultaneously falsify all of the following.

LAYER 01

Cell Tower Triangulation — Hard Gate (Not a Weighted Score)

A device's GPS position is reported by the operating system and can be overridden by any application with location permissions. Cell tower associations, by contrast, are determined by physical radio proximity to infrastructure and cannot be altered by any application — they are a function of where the device physically is, not where software claims it is.

When a device broadcasts GPS coordinates in Andheri East but is actively handing off between towers in Thane, these signals are in fundamental physical contradiction. GigShield treats this contradiction not as a penalty to the activity score, but as a hard gate: GPS-to-tower zone consistency is a non-negotiable prerequisite for payout evaluation.

IF gps_zone ≠ cell_tower_zone: activity_score = 0 claim_status = SUSPENDED reason = "Tower-GPS zone mismatch — hard gate failed" rider_notified = False // No advance warning given to spoofing actor // This gate fires BEFORE the weighted scoring model runs. // A perfect score on all other signals does not save a claim that fails here.
LAYER 02

Behavioural Biometric Fingerprint (90-Day Rolling Baseline)

Every active rider on GigShield accumulates a 90-day rolling behavioural baseline comprising: specific restaurant zones visited, average stop duration at pickup points, micro-route patterns between restaurant clusters, the cadence of zone transitions between orders, and how their activity profile varies between lunch and dinner peaks. This baseline is unique to each rider and represents their genuine working pattern.

A genuine rider stranded by heavy rain produces a recognisable disruption signature: a transition from active movement to near-stationarity within the disruption zone, at a time consistent with their usual working hours. A spoofing application simulating generic delivery-route movement lacks the hyper-local specificity of the real rider's baseline — it may produce plausible movement in general, but it will produce movement that deviates from this specific rider's fingerprint on multiple behavioural dimensions simultaneously.

The isolation forest model scores each claim event against the rider's own baseline, not a population average. This means that two riders with identical GPS traces can receive different fraud scores if one of them has never worked in that zone before.

LAYER 03

Network Environment Consistency

A device physically located in a residential area and connected to a home Wi-Fi network exhibits a measurably different network signature than the same device operating on mobile data in a high-density urban delivery zone. Network type, approximate signal strength pattern, and connection event timing are all passively logged by the GigShield Flutter app during the rider's active working hours.

Spoofing applications falsify GPS at the operating system level. They do not — and architecturally cannot — alter the physical network environment from which the device operates. A rider at home watching their GPS trace appear in Dadar will still be connected to their home router. Persistent Wi-Fi association during a claimed active delivery shift is weighted as a strong spoofing indicator in the fraud model.

LAYER 04

Cross-Signal Contradiction Score

Rather than evaluating each signal independently and summing scores, the fraud model computes a contradiction score — a measure of how internally inconsistent the available signals are with each other. A genuine rider produces a corroborating set: GPS shows movement, towers confirm movement, network shows mobile data, stop durations match restaurant pickup behaviour, and movement speed fits a two-wheeler delivery pattern. The signals tell the same story from different angles.

A spoofing attacker — even a sophisticated one — produces contradiction failures. GPS may show movement through a restaurant cluster, but the network environment shows the rider is on home Wi-Fi. The stop durations may be realistic, but the tower associations place the rider in a different district. A high contradiction score — even when no individual signal would have triggered a rejection on its own — causes immediate claim suspension.

Zone Velocity Circuit Breaker — The Syndicate Stopper
The most dangerous property of a coordinated syndicate attack is speed — 500 fraudulent claims arrive faster than any human review team can process them. The zone velocity circuit breaker is the architectural response: it requires no human intervention and operates within seconds. If claim volume in any zone spikes to 3× its 30-day rolling average within a 20-minute window, automated payouts halt entirely for that zone. The risk operations team receives an alert with the zone ID, spike magnitude, and a full list of all riders with pending claims. The circuit breaker does not discriminate between fraudulent and genuine claims — it freezes the zone and lets humans sort it out. This is the correct tradeoff: a small delay for genuine claimants is far less harmful than a drained liquidity pool.

7.3 — The UX Balance: Protecting Honest Riders

The most important design constraint in adversarial fraud defense is that the same conditions which enable fraud — poor network connectivity, unfamiliar device signals, degraded GPS in heavy rain — are also the conditions genuine riders experience during the very events GigShield is supposed to cover. A system that is too aggressive in flagging claims will wrongfully delay payments for the riders who need them most, at precisely the moment they need them. GigShield resolves this through a three-tier resolution workflow that preserves fast payouts for the majority while creating structured resolution paths for ambiguous cases.

Network Drop Tolerance During Genuine Disruptions
Severe weather events that justify a parametric payout are exactly the conditions under which mobile network coverage degrades in India. A rider in a flood-affected zone may lose GPS signal, mobile data, or both for extended periods during the disruption. GigShield accounts for this explicitly: GPS signal loss during a confirmed disruption event does not by itself reduce the activity score or trigger a fraud flag. The platform buffers the last confirmed activity state for up to 45 minutes and evaluates the claim against the pre-gap signal record. Signal gaps during confirmed disruption events are treated as environmentally expected — not as evidence of spoofing.

10 — System Architecture & Decision Engine

How the System Is Built

GigShield is a five-engine FastAPI backend connected to a Flutter mobile frontend. Every payout decision flows through a continuously evaluating decision engine that combines live external data with per-rider AI scoring.

The Five Internal Engines

Risk Engine
Evaluates zone-level disruption probability using IMD weather data, CPCB AQI feeds, and historical event logs. Feeds P(disruption) into the Pricing Engine and updates continuously during active alerts.
Activity Engine
Processes GPS telemetry, cell tower associations, network type, and stop-duration patterns from the Flutter app. Produces a composite activity score (0–100) per active rider in real time.
Fraud Engine
Runs the isolation forest anomaly model, contradiction scorer, zone velocity monitor, and cross-rider graph analysis. Operates in parallel with the Activity Engine — not sequentially — so syndicate detection fires as fast as individual fraud detection.
Pricing Engine
XGBoost two-stage model serving personalised weekly premiums. Retrains monthly on accumulated claims data. SHAP output layer generates plain-language premium breakdowns shown in the Flutter app at onboarding and each renewal.
Decision Engine
The central orchestrator. Continuously evaluates the four payout gates (disruption confirmed, activity ≥65, fraud <20%, order volume drop ≥35%). Emits a payout event when all conditions are satisfied simultaneously, or routes to the appropriate hold/review tier if they are not.
Payout System
Receives payout events from the Decision Engine. Calls the Razorpay UPI API with the rider's registered VPA. On success: updates policy ledger and triggers WhatsApp notification. On failure: retries once, then escalates to manual queue.

The Decision Engine Logic

The Decision Engine runs as a continuous evaluation loop — not a batch job. Every time any input signal changes (a new weather reading, an updated activity score, a civic alert), the engine re-evaluates all active riders in the affected zone. This is what enables the 60-second payout target: the decision is already partially evaluated before the triggering event completes.

Decision Engine — Continuous Evaluation Logic
IF ( Weather / Civic Trigger = TRUE ) API threshold breached in rider's active zone
AND ( AI Activity Score >= 65 ) Multi-signal composite from Activity Engine
AND ( Fraud Probability < 20% ) Isolation forest + contradiction score from Fraud Engine
AND ( Zone Order Volume Drop >= 35% ) Platform API proxy — confirms real disruption, not spoofed
THEN Execute Razorpay UPI Payout within 60 Seconds Ledger updated · WhatsApp sent · Audit log created
Flutter App Data Flow
The Flutter mobile app serves two roles. For riders it is the interface for onboarding, policy management, payout history, and premium payments. For the backend it is the passive telemetry source — continuously streaming GPS coordinates, cell tower associations, network type, and device attestation status to the FastAPI backend during the rider's registered working hours. No rider interaction is required to transmit these signals; they flow automatically as background data from the app's location and network services.

Why a 70–75% Loss Ratio is Sustainable
Parametric insurance products globally operate at loss ratios between 60% and 80%, reflecting the fact that they pay on objective event occurrence rather than documented individual loss. The GigShield target of 70–75% is deliberately conservative — it allocates sufficient margin for fraud reserve, technology costs, and reinsurance. At scale, the loss ratio is expected to improve further as the XGBoost models accumulate real claims data and P(disruption) estimates become more accurate at the pin-code level.

Distribution Strategy

The most capital-efficient distribution channel is a direct integration with Zomato and Swiggy — offering GigShield as an opt-in weekly benefit visible within the rider earnings section of the platform app. This channel eliminates customer acquisition cost entirely and reaches riders at the moment they are most likely to value income protection: immediately after viewing their weekly earnings. Secondary channels include WhatsApp-based community onboarding through delivery partner associations and digital kiosks at high-density pickup zones such as food courts and restaurant clusters.
